The demolition that shocked Beverly Hills

In September 2020, LeBron James paid $36.75 million for a hilltop estate at 9955 Beverly Grove Drive — a 1930s Mediterranean mansion in the Beverly Hills Post Office neighborhood. It was a significant purchase even by NBA standards. What happened next was not.

In 2023, LeBron demolished the entire original mansion. Not renovated. Not remodeled. Demolished — taking down a historic property that had once been owned by aviation tycoon Howard Hughes, leased to actress Katharine Hepburn, and later owned for decades by soap opera legends Bill and Lee Phillip Bell (creators of The Bold and the Beautiful and The Young and the Restless).

In its place, he is building not one but two entirely custom homes on the same 2.72-acre lot. Building permits reveal the plans: a primary mansion of approximately 15,906 square feet, a second home, a 7,702-square-foot basement garage, water features flanking the driveway, a spa, an outdoor kitchen, solar panels, and a private guard house. When complete in 2026, the compound is projected to be worth over $100 million — nearly three times what he paid for the land.

Why Beverly Hills Post Office?

The Beverly Hills Post Office district — abbreviated BHPO — sits above the flat grid of Beverly Hills proper. Lots are large, streets are quiet, and the elevation provides panoramic views that money can't buy anywhere else in Los Angeles. On a clear day, you can see the Pacific Ocean from LeBron's hilltop.

The Lakers' training facility in El Segundo is approximately 30 minutes away. His children's schools and his production company SpringHill Entertainment's offices are minutes from the gate. And the neighborhood's reputation for discretion — it attracts private money, not Instagram celebrities — aligns with how LeBron has quietly built his business empire away from the spotlight.

A career told in real estate

LeBron's property portfolio is a precise map of his NBA career. Every purchase mirrors a move. Every sale marks a chapter closed. No other athlete's real estate tells their story as clearly.

2003
Akron, Ohio
The Akron Mansion — LeBron's first home
$2.1M
Bought at 18 years old — the year he was drafted — in his hometown of Akron, Ohio. He razed the existing structure and built a 30,000 sq ft custom mansion with a bowling alley, recording studio, and a barbershop identical to the one he grew up visiting. Still owns it today, estimated value ~$9.2M. A deliberate act of staying connected to his roots despite becoming a global superstar.

LeBron vs. the field

Most athletes accumulate real estate. LeBron treats it as biography. Each purchase marks a moment; each demolition marks a statement.

Buying a $36.75M historic mansion and tearing it down is not a financial decision — it's a declaration. It says: I don't want to live in someone else's story. I'm building my own. The compound at Beverly Grove Drive, when complete, will be a physical manifestation of LeBron's approach to everything: don't inherit legacy, create it.

His real estate also shows a consistent thread of loyalty to his origins. He bought property in Akron at 18 and still owns it today — 23 years later. The 30,000 sq ft mansion has a barbershop identical to the one he went to as a kid. That detail says more about him than any Instagram post.
